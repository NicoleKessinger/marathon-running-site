Every marathon story starts somewhere ridiculous. For Nicole, it was 2020 — a pandemic year, a closed city, and a virtual race that meant running 26.2 miles essentially alone with a GPS watch and the quiet suspicion that this was either the bravest or most pointless thing she'd ever done. It was, as it turned out, a little of both.

The Akron Virtual Marathon was not glamorous. There was no crowd noise, no water table camaraderie, no stranger handing you a warm pretzel at mile 20. There was just the road, the mileage, and the finish — earned in full, even if no one was watching.

“From below average to slightly above it — one finish line at a time.”

By 2022, the roads reopened. Columbus offered a proper race-day experience: crowds, corrals, and the particular chaos of 20,000 people who all believe they trained enough. It was Nicole's first in-person marathon — noisier, harder, and significantly better than the living room version.

2023 brought a return to Akron — this time in person, and this time with more miles in the legs and more respect for the distance. Then came 2025, and with it, the one that changes everything: the TCS New York City Marathon, one of the six World Marathon Majors, and the finish line on Central Park South that every runner quietly dreams about.

On the Distance

The marathon doesn't negotiate. It offers the same 26.2 miles to everyone and asks only that you show up. Every race in this timeline was finished — not fast, not always pretty, but finished. That matters more than the clock.

What changes across four races isn't the distance. It's the runner. The relationship with the miles shifts from survival to something closer to understanding — not comfort, exactly, but familiarity with the hurt.

What NYC Meant

New York is the marathon that athletes talk about like a destination rather than a race. Five boroughs, two million spectators, and a final mile through Central Park that has humbled far faster people than most of us will ever be.

To line up in Staten Island and finish in Manhattan is to have earned something. The results are linked above. But the real result is a runner who started in a pandemic with a GPS watch and ended up on one of the world's great stages — slightly above average, right on schedule.
