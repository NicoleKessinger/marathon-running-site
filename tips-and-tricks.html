Gear • Nutrition • Field-Tested Hacks

What I Wish Someone
Had Told Me at Mile One

Everything I've learned across four marathons about shoes, fuel, and the small tricks that make the difference between surviving and finishing strong.

The running world will sell you a lot of things. Miracle shoes. Protein formulas. GPS watches that cost more than your first car. Most of it is noise. What actually matters to a beginner is far simpler: the right shoes, a reliable fuel strategy, and a short list of hard-won hacks that no one puts in the training plan.

After four marathons, two of them in Akron and one in New York City, here is the gear that stayed in the rotation and the tricks that saved miles I would otherwise have lost to blisters, bonking, and bad decisions.

Editor's Note: These are personal recommendations based on real race experience, not sponsorships. No shoe company has ever given me free shoes. I'm still waiting for that to change.

01

Shoes

The Only Gear That Actually Matters
Top Pick — Beginners
Neutral • Daily Trainer
Brooks Ghost 16
Brooks Running

The most forgiving daily trainer on the market. Soft, consistent cushioning that works on pavement, treadmill, and tired legs alike. The shoe you wear for 80% of your training runs.

Max Cushion • Long Runs
Hoka Clifton 9
Hoka

Pillow-like underfoot feel that becomes essential once your long runs pass 14 miles. Unusually light for the stack height. If your joints are talking to you, these will quiet them down.

Stability • Mild Overpronator
ASICS Gel-Kayano 31
ASICS

The benchmark stability shoe for runners who need medial support. Built for high mileage. If a running store tells you that you overpronate, this is the conversation you need to have.

Versatile • Best Value
Saucony Ride 17
Saucony

Balanced cushion, good durability, reasonable price. The car that always starts — not flashy, completely dependable. An excellent second shoe once you're rotating two pairs.

Race Day • Speed
New Balance SuperComp Trainer
New Balance

Carbon-plate energy return without the brutal stiffness of true race shoes. A beginner's gateway into plated footwear for those who want to feel what fast actually feels like.

Before you buy anything: Visit a specialty running store for a gait analysis. It's free, takes 10 minutes, and will save you from six weeks of shin splints. Replace shoes every 300–500 miles. And never — ever — race in shoes you haven't trained in.

02

Gels & Nutrition

Fuel Before You're Hungry
GU Energy Gel
Classic Energy Gel • 100 cal

The industry standard. 28 flavors, widely available at aid stations. Start here. Take with water, never Gatorade — the combination can cause stomach upset.

Maurten Gel 100
Hydrogel • Easy on Stomach

What the elites use. Hydrogel technology means almost no GI distress. Tasteless and textureless — a revelation if you struggle with traditional gels. More expensive, worth it on race day.

Honey Stinger Gel
Natural • Organic Honey Base

For runners who prefer real ingredients over lab formulas. Organic honey base with B vitamins. Slightly thicker consistency but smooth taste. Good for those sensitive to artificial flavors.

CLIF Bloks
Chewable • 33 cal per block

Chewable cubes for runners who gag on gels. Take two blocks every 30 minutes. Easier to portion, easier to carry, and psychologically easier for many beginners to manage mid-run.

6

The Fueling Rule: Start at Mile 6, Not Mile 16

Don't wait until you feel depleted to take your first gel. By then it's too late. Take your first gel at mile 6–8, then every 45 minutes after. Carry your own — never rely on aid station supplies matching your planned fuel. Practice every gel flavor in training before you take it on race day.

03

Running Hacks

Field-Tested, Blister-Approved
1
Body Glide is non-negotiable

Apply Body Glide to your inner thighs, underarms, anywhere clothing rubs, and the back of your heels before every run over 6 miles. Chafing at mile 20 is not a character-building experience. It is just pain.

2
Lace your shoes for your feet

If your toenails are black after long runs, your laces are too tight at the toe box. Look up "heel lock lacing" — a simple technique that locks your heel in place and prevents your foot from sliding forward downhill.

3
Freeze your water bottles

For summer long runs, freeze your handheld water bottles halfway and top them up before you leave. You'll have cold water for 12–14 miles without needing a hydration vest. Works best in bottles without a straw.

4
Run-walk is a legitimate strategy

Jeff Galloway's run-walk method — run 4 minutes, walk 1 minute — has helped thousands of beginners finish marathons they wouldn't have otherwise finished. Walking is not failure. Strategic walking is racing smarter.

5
Walk every uphill over 6%

The energy cost of running a steep hill versus power-hiking it is significant. Save those leg muscles. Walk uphills aggressively in the first half of a race and you'll have fuel left when everyone else is walking the flats.

6
Run your easy days easy

The biggest mistake most beginners make is running every run at the same moderate effort. Easy runs should feel almost embarrassingly slow — conversational pace. This is where your aerobic base is built. Save the effort for long runs and tempo days.

7
Safety pin all four corners of your bib

Most runners pin two corners. When a bib flaps for 26.2 miles in the wind, it sounds like you're being chased by a very small flag. Four pins. Every time. Your fellow runners will thank you, and so will your race photos.

8
Nothing new on race day

No new shoes. No new socks. No new food, no new gel flavor, no new shorts. Every item you wear or consume on race day should have been tested on at least two long runs. Race day is not the day to experiment.

9
Compression socks reduce soreness

Wear graduated compression socks — knee-high — during and after long runs. They improve circulation, reduce swelling, and meaningfully shorten recovery time. CEP and Swiftwick are the brands worth owning.

10
Sleep is the best recovery tool

More valuable than ice baths, more effective than massage guns, cheaper than everything. 8–9 hours the night before a long run and 8–9 hours after. Your body does not rebuild during the run. It rebuilds during sleep.

Race Day • Complete Checklist

Don't Leave the Hotel Without It

The Night Before
  • Race bib (pinned, all 4 corners)
  • Timing chip on shoe
  • Outfit laid out (tested shoes)
  • Gels pre-counted & loaded
  • Alarm set (×2)
  • Nothing new in the bag
Race Morning
  • Breakfast 2–3 hrs before gun
  • Body Glide applied
  • Sunscreen (if warm)
  • Hydrate — 16 oz water
  • Bag drop packed
  • Know the corral location
What to Carry
  • 4–6 gels minimum
  • Salt tabs (warm weather)
  • Phone + headphones
  • ID (always)
  • Small cash / transit card
  • Throwaway warm layer
Finish Line Bag
  • Dry change of clothes
  • Flip flops or slides
  • Snacks & electrolyte drink
  • Compression socks
  • Foam roller (hotel room)
  • Celebrate. You earned it.
