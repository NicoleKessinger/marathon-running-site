Beginner's Guide • Step by Step • 28 Weeks to 26.2

Every Marathon Starts
With a Single Walk

The honest, no-nonsense guide to going from the couch to the start line — without injuring yourself in the first week.

1
Start with walking

Before you run a single step, walk. A 20–30 minute brisk walk builds the aerobic base, strengthens your joints, and teaches your body what sustained movement feels like. Walk for the first full week — no exceptions.

2
Add a short jog

Once walking feels comfortable, alternate: walk 4 minutes, jog 1 minute. Repeat. The jog should feel almost embarrassingly slow — if you can't hold a conversation, you're going too fast. Effort, not pace, is the measure.

3
Rest between runs

Recovery is not laziness — it's training. Your muscles, tendons, and bones adapt to running stress during rest days. Never run on back-to-back days as a beginner. Aim for 3 days of running per week with full rest or easy walking between.

4
Stretch after — not before

Save static stretching (holding a stretch for 20–30 seconds) for after your run, when muscles are warm. Before running, do 3–5 minutes of dynamic movement: leg swings, hip circles, gentle lunges. Cold muscles don't like being pulled.

5
Stop if it hurts

Learn the difference between discomfort and pain. Burning lungs and tired legs are normal. Sharp pain in a joint, shin, or foot is a signal — stop immediately. Running through pain doesn't build toughness; it builds injuries that cost you weeks.

3

Days Per Week — The Beginner Rule

Three running days per week is the sweet spot for beginners: enough stimulus to improve, enough rest to recover. Monday, Wednesday, Saturday is a proven structure. Add a 4th day only after completing at least 6 weeks at 3 days without soreness or injury.
