History • Origins • Legacy

From a Battlefield Messenger
to a Global Movement

The marathon's 2,500-year journey — from a desperate run across ancient Greece to the world's most beloved endurance race.

490 BC

Pheidippides, an Athenian herald, runs approximately 25 miles from the battlefield of Marathon to Athens to announce the Greek victory over Persia. According to legend, he delivered the message "Nike!" (victory) and collapsed. The myth of the marathon was born.

1896

The first modern Olympic Games are held in Athens, Greece. Inspired by the legend of Pheidippides, a long-distance race is included — the original Olympic Marathon. Greek water-carrier Spyridon Louis wins in 2:58:50, becoming a national hero.

1908

At the London Olympics, the race distance is set at exactly 26 miles 385 yards — or 26.2 miles — so the course can start beneath the Royal Family's nursery window at Windsor Castle and finish in front of the Royal Box at Olympic Stadium. The distance sticks forever.

1966

Roberta Gibb becomes the first woman to run the Boston Marathon — unofficially, hiding in bushes near the start until the pack passed. She completed all 26.2 miles without an official entry. Her run ignited a movement that would change the sport forever.

1972

After decades of exclusion, women are officially permitted to race in Olympic Marathon competition. The fight was long — in 1967, Kathrine Switzer famously ran the Boston Marathon under the name "K.V. Switzer" after officials tried to physically remove her from the course. Her courage changed the sport.

Today

Marathon running has exploded into a global movement. Over 1 million runners cross marathon finish lines every year across the world — from New York and London to Tokyo and Berlin. What began as a single desperate run now unites people of every age, background, and pace. Including us.
